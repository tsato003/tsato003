About Me

I am Tetsuya Sato, a graduate student who is pursuing a PhD degree in human factors psychology at Old Dominion Univeristy. I have a bachelor's of science degree and a master's of science degree in psychology. Currently, I am working as a graduate research assistant for the Applied Cognitive Performance Laboratory. Also, I work as a graduate teaching assistant for PSYC 316 and PSYC 201s. My research interest includes human-automation interaction, trust, attention allocation, and eye movement behaivor.

Current Research

Advanced Air Mobility (AAM) is a set of emerging technologies that enable mobility of goods and people in urban and rural areas. While AAM has a potential to transform logistics and traffic in the U.S. air zones, a significant increase in automation is required to enable the range of AAM operations. The human performance literature indicates that human operators are particularly poor at sustaining attention to a perceptually demanding tasks over the course of even a half hour or less, a phenomenon termed the vigilance decrement. Increasing levels of automation in the AAM domain will alter the operatorâ€™s role from controlling to monitoring the system, and thus, ironically attentional performance may decline while interacting with highly reliable automated systems. However, research directly examining human attention system during interaction with automation is unfortunately scant, a research gap that the current proposal aims to fill. The present proposal aims to evaluate two models of vigilance decrements in the AAM operation environment.
